Fleet View.

Designing the interface that made it possible for one operator to safely manage an entire drone fleet — and giving regulators the evidence they needed to allow it.

The project § 01

Fleet View is the command environment for operators running many drones at once.

The business problem was simple: more drones, same number of pilots. The design problem was harder — how do you keep one person genuinely safe across ten simultaneous flights?

As FlytBase deployments scaled — more docks, fewer pilots per site, alarm integrations firing at any hour — operators found themselves responsible for fleets their existing interface wasn't built for. Fleet View was the product response: a dedicated environment where one operator could observe, manage, and execute safety controls across every active flight simultaneously.

And because CAA bodies were beginning to grant One-to-Many waivers — permitting a single licensed pilot to command multiple armed drones concurrently — the design also had to function as regulatory evidence of adequate situational awareness. The interface wasn't just a product. It was part of a safety case.

The challenge § 02

Two different people. Completely different needs. One interface that couldn't afford to fail either of them.

Every ROC (Remote Operations Centre) has two roles that need Fleet View, and they need it differently. The pilot is operational — dispatching drones, responding to alarms, taking control under pressure. They need the ability to go deep on one drone without losing awareness of the rest.

The supervisor is responsible for the complete operation — fleet state, pilot assignments, brief telemetry across every drone in the air. They're watching, not commanding — until a critical situation demands otherwise.

One interface. Two mental models. Zero tolerance for losing sight of a drone — because at any moment, either role might need to step into what the other was doing.

The central decision § 03

Group, don't filter.

Standard data tables let you filter rows out. In a drone fleet dashboard, that's the wrong instinct.

The obvious move when an interface gets crowded is to let the user filter — show me only the drones at Site A, hide the idle ones, only show alerts. But filtering removes drones from awareness. And in a regulated environment, that's exactly what a CAA evaluator does not want to see. An operator can't claim situational awareness over a drone they filtered off their screen.

So we took a different approach: grouping instead of filtering. Every drone stays visible. Every drone stays in the operator's field of attention. Grouping lets the operator collapse and expand without ever losing a drone from the count. Armed drones? Grouped, but the group count and status indicator remain prominent. Idle fleet? Collapsed, but the number of idle drones is explicit at the header level.

This was a product decision before it was a design decision. It required understanding what filtering costs in this domain — and being willing to challenge a pattern that works everywhere else.

The interface § 04

Four panels, always on. Every panel talking to every other panel.

Fleet View is organised around four simultaneous panels — list, map, video wall, and alerts — each answering a different question but all sharing state.

The list is the spine: every drone, grouped by state. The map shows where they are. The video wall tiles live feeds by group priority. The alerts panel surfaces everything that needs attention, sorted by severity.

Crucially: selecting a drone in any panel highlights it everywhere. Clicking an alert highlights the affected drone on the map, lights the border of its video feed, and scrolls the list to it. This cross-panel synchronisation is what makes one interface function for both pilot and supervisor — the depth of focus is up to the operator, but the fleet never disappears.

The alarm flow § 05

The hardest scenario in fleet ops — designed as a first-class workflow.

Alarm dispatch — an emergency on one drone while nine others are flying — is the moment everything in the interface has to work at once. It couldn't be a side path from the main monitoring view. It had to be a native, ergonomic workflow.

When an alarm fires, Fleet View does three things simultaneously: the alert slides into the panel with its severity already encoded; the affected drone glows on the map and its video border lights; and the pilot gets a one-click path to take direct control without losing the fleet overview behind them. No modal dialog swallowing the screen. No context-switch where the rest of the fleet disappears.

The supervisor sees the same event unfold in real time — same highlights, same state, same clock. If they need to intervene, the same controls are one click away. The interface never hides the fact that the rest of the fleet is still flying.

What I took away § 07

Challenging a universal pattern is a product decision, not a design one. "Grouping not filtering" wasn't a visual choice. It required understanding what filtering would cost in this domain — and being willing to challenge a pattern that works everywhere else. That's a product call, and it had to be made before any interface was designed.

Design can be regulatory infrastructure. When a CAA evaluator asks whether an operator has adequate situational awareness, the interface is the answer. Knowing that changed the stakes of every structural decision — the grouping model, the priority hierarchy, the always-visible safety controls all had to work as proof, not just as features.

The most important moment in a product is the one you design for first. Building the alarm dispatch workflow as a first-class, integrated flow — not as a side path from the main monitoring view — shaped every subsequent layout and panel decision. Design the hardest moment first. Everything else will fit around it.
