Asset Management.

Designing FlytBase's first product that isn't about the flight.

Role
Lead Product Designer

Context § 01

FlytBase had always been flight-first. This module wasn't.

Cockpit, Fleet, Mission Planning, Scheduler — every surface FlytBase had shipped until now was about the flight. The path the drone takes. The waypoints. The commands. Asset Management was the first time we were building a product that doesn't care about the flight.

Sarah Martinez runs operations at Devon Energy. She's responsible for 45+ wellheads spread across 15 square miles in North Cowden Unit. She schedules inspections, coordinates with maintenance teams, and files monthly compliance reports to regulators. She uses drones because they're faster and safer than sending people, but drones aren't her job. The wellheads are her job.

On the existing FlytBase dashboard, she could fly missions. What she couldn't do was answer the question her boss asked her every week: "when was wellhead NC-0217 last inspected?" The answer existed, somewhere, in a mission log she'd have to find by name. The software she used every day didn't know what a wellhead was.

The problem § 02

You could fly missions. You couldn't track places.

The existing system recorded flights beautifully — every waypoint, every telemetry reading, every piece of captured media. But infrastructure only existed as a string inside a mission's name. "Wellhead NC-0217 - Monthly Inspection" was the only thread tying a place to a flight, and that thread broke the moment someone mistyped the name, changed the naming convention, or inspected the same asset under a different mission.

There was no list of Sarah's wellheads anywhere in the product. No inspection history per asset. No way to see how a wellhead's condition had changed over six months. No templates for "here's how you always inspect this type of thing." Every monthly report Sarah filed, she built from scratch by scavenging across dozens of mission logs.

The gap wasn't missing features. It was a missing abstraction. The software didn't have a concept for the thing the user actually cared about.

The shift § 03

Track the thing. Not the flight.

This sounds small. It wasn't. Every product surface FlytBase had shipped was flight-first — and this new module had to sit alongside them, in the same authentication, the same navigation, the same customer account, but operate on a completely different axis.

The move: create a second dashboard. Not a tab inside Operations. Not a new view. A separate product, launched from the FlytBase hamburger menu, with its own data model centred on Assets. An asset is a record — a wellhead, a power line, a pipeline, a generator — with its own ID, its own inspection history, its own media library, its own alert thresholds, its own reference imagery. Flights don't disappear. But they stop being the centre of the universe.

The Operations Dashboard continues to exist and continues to fly drones. But increasingly, everything you do in Operations now references an asset. You plan missions around assets. You launch GoToAsset instead of GoToLocation. You execute a Quick Asset Inspect instead of building a mission from scratch. The flight becomes a means. The asset is the end.

The payoff § 04

Once assets exist, templates become possible.

Sarah has 45 wellheads. They're mostly the same. The drone needs to fly the same pattern around each — four viewpoints, a fixed altitude, the same lens at the same zoom, the same gimbal attitude. Without an asset abstraction, she'd configure 45 missions identically. With one, she configures a profile once and reuses it 45 times.

Inspection Profiles are reusable viewpoint definitions. Two types: Grid (for area-coverage inspections — area, GSD, overlap, photogrammetry settings) and Viewpoints (for discrete inspection points — waypoints, lens, zoom, gimbal attitude, per-waypoint actions). One profile, applied to dozens of similar assets, collapses setup time from hours to minutes.

The same templatization logic applies to Alert Profiles — a wellhead has different abnormal-temperature thresholds than a substation, which has different thresholds than a pipeline. Each asset type gets its own alert profile library. The software knows the rules by asset type, so Sarah doesn't have to re-teach it every time.

The module § 05

Five pages. One coherent model.

The dashboard has five top-level pages, organised as a left-rail navigation. Each is the same underlying data sliced a different way — by asset, by flight, by media, by report, by tag.

Assets — the inventory. Import 1000+ assets via KML or CSV. Organise by site, by sector, by tags. Search, filter, group. The map on the right reflects whatever selection is active.

Inspection Logs — cross-asset flight history. Every flight that happened anywhere in the system, searchable and filterable. Click into one and see its media, its map path, its asset context.

Media — every piece of captured imagery, chronologically, tagged, annotated. The Gallery tab for Sarah's team when they're reviewing evidence across dozens of flights.

Reports — the stakeholder-facing surface. Compliance reports, maintenance reports, custom reports — generated from asset data, distributed as PDF, secure link, or QR code.

Tag Management — tags aren't strings you type, they're records. A dedicated page to create them, merge them, rename them, retire them. Because when you have 1000+ assets, tag sprawl is a real operational problem.

What shipped § 06

M1 went live as a second product.

Asset Management shipped as a standalone dashboard inside the FlytBase ecosystem — same authentication, same customer account, separate surface. It's the first FlytBase module that isn't about flying a drone.

What I learnt § 07

Sometimes the problem isn't a feature. It's an abstraction. No number of improvements to mission logs would have solved Sarah's problem, because her problem was that the software didn't have a concept for the thing she cared about. Finding the right abstraction was the work. Everything downstream followed from it.

Shifts like this compound. Once assets exist, templates become possible. Once templates exist, setup time collapses. Once data is structured by asset, reports become queries. Once Operations references assets, GoToAsset replaces GoToLocation. Each downstream win was free — we got it by making one decision correctly early.

A product company can enter new categories from a strong base. Asset Management isn't a pivot. It's a second product that sits next to the first, serves the same customers, and expands what FlytBase is. That's a different kind of work — less refinement, more invention — and it's the kind I want to keep doing.
